Is the Hermès Birkin Bag a Better Investment than Stocks or Gold?

A Practical Data Science for Economists Project

Aim of the Project

In contrast to more conventional assets like gold and the S&P 500, the project seeks to evaluate the Hermès Birkin bag's potential as a financial investment. In order to determine if luxury handbags can be reliable, profitable investments, this study examines price growth, inflation-adjusted values, volatility, and cumulative returns. The study also looks into how market demand, exclusivity, and scarcity affect Birkin bags' worth over time, providing information on their place in diverse portfolios.

Data Used

PurseBop, Baghunter, and other premium resale websites provided the data on Birkin bags. Nasdaq and MacroTrends were used to retrieve historical S&P 500 and gold values. MacroTrends and IMF projections were used to determine inflation rates. Python's pandas module was used at Google Colab to handle the data for cleaning, adjusting for inflation, and computing cumulative returns. All datasets were transformed into standardized formats for analysis, and automated scripts guaranteed replicability.

Challenges in Data Cleaning and Analysis

The inconsistent Birkin bag pricing data, which resulted from model-specific variances and a lack of public records, was a major obstacle. Average prices for similar models were determined in order to resolve this. Accurate historical rates had to be sourced for inflation adjustments, and currency disputes were settled by converting to USD. Using Python packages, Google Colab offered a productive environment for data cleansing and standardization.

Chart 4: Supply vs. Demand of Birkin Bags

This chart highlights the supply and demand disparity for Hermès Birkin bags, emphasizing how exclusivity and scarcity drive resale values.

Data Sources:

  • Supply Estimates: Annual Birkin production is estimated at 12,000–70,000 bags globally. Read more on Madison Avenue Couture .
  • Price Data: Retail and resale prices are sourced from Saclab . Rare models, like the Niloticus Himalaya, resell for $154,000—triple their store price.
  • Data Processing: Prices in Euros were converted to USD using a 1.1 exchange rate in Google Colab .

Insights:

  • Supply vs. Demand Pricing: Retail prices are consistently lower than resale values. For instance, the "Regular Leather in a Classic Colour" retails at $8,800 but resells for $14,300.
  • Rarity Drives Demand: Rare models, such as the Niloticus Himalaya, see disproportionately higher resale prices, underscoring the value of exotic materials and scarcity.
  • Exclusivity Strategy: Hermès limits supply to maintain demand and resale value, making Birkins a desirable and appreciating asset.

This chart illustrates how scarcity and desirability elevate the Birkin's status as a luxury investment, with resale values far exceeding retail prices.

Conclusion

In terms of stability and returns, Birkin bags do better than gold and the S&P 500.
